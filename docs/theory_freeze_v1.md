## 2. Final Core Research Question

### Primary Research Question

When does geopolitical competition generate positive market reactions for strategically important firms or sectors through expected state support?

### Academic Version

Under what conditions do financial markets interpret geopolitical competition as a source of state-backed opportunity rather than only as geopolitical risk?

### Portfolio / GitHub Version

This project studies when geopolitical competition causes markets to reward strategically important firms because investors expect subsidies, procurement, protection, or other state support.

## 3. Final Puzzle

The traditional geopolitical risk view expects market losses:

> Geopolitical competition -> uncertainty / risk -> market losses

This logic is usually correct at the aggregate level. Rivalry, war, sanctions, export controls, and strategic decoupling can raise uncertainty, disrupt supply chains, reduce market access, and increase compliance costs.

But the case comparison shows that some firms and sectors may experience positive market reactions. The revised argument is:

> Geopolitical competition -> strategic importance -> state support -> market opportunity

The puzzle is therefore not whether geopolitical competition is harmful. It often is. The puzzle is why selected firms may gain when states convert geopolitical pressure into subsidies, procurement, regulatory protection, financing, industrial policy, or strategic capacity commitments.

The project matters because it identifies a missing step in the geopolitical risk literature. Markets may not only price danger. They may also price expected state intervention for strategically important actors.

## 4. Final Theory

Geopolitical competition changes the political and economic value of certain firms, sectors, technologies, and locations. Firms in semiconductors, defence, energy security, AI infrastructure, critical minerals, telecommunications, or supply-chain chokepoints may become strategically important because states view them as necessary for national security, technological leadership, resilience, or alliance competition.

Strategic importance by itself does not guarantee positive market reactions. A strategically important firm may still lose export markets, face restrictions, absorb costly compliance burdens, or be forced into inefficient investment. Strategic importance is therefore a scope condition, not the core causal mechanism. It identifies where states are likely to care, but it does not by itself explain why investors should expect gains.

The core mechanism is state support. When governments recognise strategic importance, they may provide support through subsidies, procurement, tax credits, public loans, guarantees, regulation, protection, local-content rules, industrial policy, or direct capacity investment. This support can convert geopolitical vulnerability into expected revenue, lower capital costs, protected demand, or reduced downside risk.

Investors then interpret the event. A geopolitical shock may send both a threat signal and an opportunity signal. The threat signal points to uncertainty, restricted markets, retaliation, input disruption, or compliance costs. The opportunity signal points to state-backed support, procurement demand, protected market position, or strategic investment. Positive market reactions should be most likely when the opportunity signal is credible, material, and attached to identifiable firms or sectors.

The theory therefore does not claim that risk disappears. Positive market reactions occur because state-backed opportunity becomes more salient than, or sufficiently offsets, the threat channel for specific firms. The effect should be cross-sectional: some firms gain, some lose, and broad market effects may remain negative.

The strongest version of the theory is narrow:

> Geopolitical competition generates positive market reactions when strategically important firms or sectors are expected to receive credible state support that improves future cash flows or reduces downside risk.

## 6. Main Concepts

## Geopolitical Competition

Rivalry among states that uses military, economic, technological, regulatory, or industrial-policy tools to pursue security, influence, autonomy, or strategic advantage. In this project, the relevant events are not generic tensions but policy-relevant actions such as export controls, subsidies, procurement, strategic investment, industrial policy, defence spending, and supply-chain restrictions.

## Strategic Importance

The degree to which a firm, sector, technology, or location is viewed by governments as important for national security, technological leadership, economic resilience, military capability, or control over critical supply chains. Strategic importance is a scope condition: it makes state support more likely, but does not by itself imply market gains.

## State Support

Government action that plausibly improves the expected economic position of strategically important firms or sectors. It includes subsidies, procurement, grants, tax credits, loans, guarantees, regulation, protection, industrial policy, local-content rules, trusted-supplier rules, and strategic capacity commitments.

## Market Opportunity

An expected improvement in firm or sector value arising from state-backed support, protected demand, reduced downside risk, substitution demand, or strategic investment. Market opportunity refers to investor expectations and market reactions, not necessarily long-term welfare or realized productivity.

## Investor Interpretation

The process by which investors classify a geopolitical event as a threat, an opportunity, or a mixed signal for particular firms or sectors. Investor interpretation matters because geopolitical events do not mechanically produce one market response.

## Strategic Importance Premium

A positive market valuation effect for firms or sectors perceived as strategically important and likely to receive credible state support. The premium should not be expected from strategic importance alone; it should appear when investors expect state support or protection to affect future cash flows or risk.

## 7. Scope Conditions

## Where the Theory Should Apply Best

- Strategic industries where governments have clear security or resilience concerns.
- Semiconductors and semiconductor manufacturing equipment.
- Defence firms, ammunition producers, and military technology suppliers.
- Energy security assets, especially where state policy supports capacity, storage, or trusted supply.
- AI infrastructure, including chips, compute, cloud, power, cooling, and data centres, when linked to national-power policy rather than generic AI hype.
- Critical minerals and processing capacity where supply dependence is viewed as a security risk.
- Telecommunications infrastructure and trusted-vendor policy.
- Supply-chain chokepoints where states seek reshoring, friend-shoring, redundancy, or trusted capacity.
- Firms or economies central to national-security strategy, alliance commitments, or strategic industrial policy.

## Where the Theory May Not Apply

- Broad market indices during major war or crisis shocks, where risk-off dynamics dominate.
- Firms in strategic sectors that receive no credible support.
- Firms harmed by export controls, sanctions, or retaliation without offsetting support.
- Sectors where state support is symbolic, delayed, underfunded, or politically non-credible.
- Commodity price shocks where gains are better explained by supply-demand mechanics than state support.
- Events that are fully anticipated before the announcement.
- Cases where support imposes costs that outweigh benefits, such as inefficient domestic investment, guardrails, political interference, or compliance burdens.

## 8. Hypotheses

## H1 State Support Hypothesis

Geopolitical competition events that include credible state support will produce more positive market reactions for affected firms or sectors than events that only increase geopolitical tension.

## H2 Strategic Importance Scope Hypothesis

State support will have stronger positive market effects when directed toward strategically important firms or sectors than when directed toward less strategically important firms or sectors.

## H3 Threat Dominance Hypothesis

When geopolitical events impose direct restrictions, lost market access, retaliation risk, or input disruption without credible offsetting state support, threat signals will dominate and market reactions will be negative.

## H4 Substitution / Reallocation Hypothesis

Geopolitical restrictions will produce positive market reactions for alternative suppliers or trusted-location beneficiaries when the event plausibly redirects demand, sourcing, or investment toward them.

## H5 Adaptation / Learning Hypothesis

As similar geopolitical-policy events repeat, market reactions will become more differentiated between threatened firms and state-supported or strategically positioned beneficiaries.

## 9. What This Theory Is NOT Saying

- It does not claim all geopolitical risk is good.
- It does not claim geopolitical competition creates aggregate market gains.
- It does not claim state support always creates positive returns.
- It does not claim strategic importance alone guarantees a premium.
- It does not claim market reactions are rational, accurate, or permanent.
- It does not claim causality can be proven easily with event studies.
- It does not reduce everything to subsidies; state support also includes procurement, protection, financing, regulation, and strategic capacity commitments.
- It does not claim that investor opportunity is the same as social welfare.
- It does not claim that positive market reactions imply successful industrial policy.

## 10. Observable Implications

If the theory is correct, we should observe:

- Positive abnormal returns around events that announce credible state support for strategic firms or sectors.
- Stronger positive reactions for named or clearly eligible beneficiaries than for broad sector peers.
- More positive reactions to direct support events than to symbolic strategic statements.
- Negative reactions to restriction-only events for directly exposed firms.
- Mixed reactions to export controls: direct losers should fall, while credible substitutes or protected alternatives may gain.
- Stronger market reactions when support is specific, funded, and near-term rather than vague or rhetorical.
- Weaker opportunity effects when state support is anticipated, politically uncertain, or attached to costly conditions.
- Strategic importance functioning as an amplifier of state support, not as an independent guarantee of gains.
- Investor or firm narratives framing supported firms as critical, protected, subsidized, trusted, or procurement-relevant.
- Cross-sectional divergence within the same geopolitical event rather than uniform sector-wide movement.

## 11. Falsification Conditions

The theory would be weakened or falsified if:

- State-support events do not generate positive reactions for named or clearly eligible beneficiaries.
- Strategic firms react no differently from non-strategic firms after support announcements.
- Restriction-only events produce the same positive reactions as funded support events.
- Positive reactions occur only in broad sector rallies unrelated to geopolitical policy.
- Opportunity effects disappear after using sector benchmarks.
- Market reactions are better explained by earnings, interest rates, commodity prices, or technology cycles.
- Strategic importance predicts neither state support nor positive market response.
- State support is consistently interpreted as a sign of firm weakness rather than opportunity.
- Substitution or reallocation beneficiaries cannot be identified before observing returns.
- Repeated events do not produce clearer differentiation between threatened and supported firms.

The most damaging result would be:

> Once market and sector effects are controlled, credible state support for strategically important firms has no positive association with abnormal returns.

## 12. Recommended Empirical Focus

The first master's-level empirical test should focus on:

> **Semiconductor subsidies and strategic industrial-policy announcements.**

This is the best first path because it matches the frozen theory most directly. The theory now centres on state support, and semiconductor industrial policy provides observable support events: CHIPS Act milestones, preliminary funding terms, state-backed fab announcements, and related strategic-capacity commitments. These events identify beneficiaries more clearly than broad export controls and are less conceptually thin than generic defence-spending reactions.

Export controls remain useful as contrast cases, but they should not be the first empirical focus. They are often dominated by threat signals, anticipated policy sequences, and mixed exposure. They are better for testing the threat dominance and substitution hypotheses after the state-support mechanism is established.

Defence spending shocks are feasible but less novel. A finding that defence firms rise after procurement or defence-spending announcements would be empirically straightforward but theoretically weaker.

Cross-sector comparison is attractive for a longer project but too broad for a master's thesis. It risks reintroducing the conceptual sprawl that this freeze is meant to prevent.

The recommended first test is therefore:

> Compare market reactions around semiconductor state-support announcements for named beneficiaries, sector peers, and broad benchmarks, while using export-control events as a limited contrast group for threat-dominant geopolitical shocks.

This gives the project a focused empirical target, a clear mechanism, and a realistic master's-level path.
