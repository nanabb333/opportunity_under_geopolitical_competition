# Interview Story V2

## Why Did You Build Repo 1?

I built `opportunity_under_geopolitical_competition` to turn my dissertation research into a reproducible analytical foundation.

The original problem was academic: how do financial markets interpret geopolitical competition, especially when states respond with industrial policy or support for strategic sectors?

I did not want the project to stop as a paper. I wanted to show that a research question could become:

- a structured dataset
- a documented event taxonomy
- a deterministic similarity engine
- scenario analysis
- observed pathway summaries
- analyst briefs
- a reproducible dashboard

The goal was not to predict markets. The goal was to show disciplined evidence synthesis.

## Why Did You Build Repo 2?

I built `Taiwan_Geopolitical_Risk_Project` to move from broad dissertation research toward a focused analytics engine.

Taiwan is a natural case because geopolitical risk, semiconductor supply chains, industrial policy, and military pressure intersect there. The project gives the portfolio a sharper regional and analytical focus.

The purpose of Repo 2 is to show that the research framework can be operationalised around a specific risk domain.

## Why Did You Build Repo 3?

I built `AI_Geopolitical_Risk_Dashboard` to turn the analytics into a decision-support product experience.

Research and analytics are valuable only if they can be communicated clearly. Repo 3 is meant to show how a user might interact with scenario evidence, risk signals, or analytic summaries through a dashboard or assistant-style interface.

The product boundary matters: this is decision support, not automated prediction or investment advice.

## What Did You Learn?

I learned that the hardest part of geopolitical analytics is not only building a model or dashboard. The harder problem is preserving evidence quality while making outputs useful.

Specific lessons:

- Research framing prevents dashboards from becoming shallow.
- Dataset governance matters when evidence is qualitative.
- Historical analogues are useful only when limitations are visible.
- Automation should support review, not replace it.
- Decision-support tools need traceability, not just clean UI.

## What Would You Improve Next?

The next improvement would be expanding the evidence base and strengthening the connection between source monitoring, candidate review, and approved dataset updates.

Near-term improvements:

- add more historical events
- improve event family balance
- expand regional coverage
- refine source reliability scoring
- add stronger candidate review tooling

Longer-term improvements:

- build a controlled source-monitoring workflow
- add citation-aware evidence retrieval
- create user-authored scenario profiles
- add LLM assistance only after the evidence pipeline is stable and auditable

## Interview Summary

The concise interview story is:

> I started with dissertation research, converted it into structured historical evidence, built deterministic analytics on top of it, and then packaged the results into decision-support interfaces. The portfolio shows that I can move from research to data to automation to product while keeping limitations clear.
