## Clean Revised Chapter 3 Text

### Chapter 3. Theory

#### 3.1 Introduction

This chapter presents the theoretical framework for the dissertation. The central question is when geopolitical competition generates positive market reactions for strategically important firms or sectors through expected state support. The theory does not claim that geopolitical competition is generally beneficial for markets. It begins from the opposite premise: geopolitical competition often increases uncertainty, raises compliance costs, restricts market access, disrupts supply chains, and produces negative aggregate market effects, consistent with the geopolitical-risk literature (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022). The puzzle is that selected firms may nevertheless experience positive market reactions when geopolitical competition leads states to provide credible support to strategically important actors. The argument is organised around a conditional pathway: Geopolitical Competition → Strategic Importance → State Support → Investor Interpretation → Positive Market Reaction.

The mechanism is state support. Strategic importance identifies which firms, sectors, technologies, or locations become salient to governments under geopolitical competition. However, strategic importance alone does not guarantee positive market reactions. A strategically important firm may still face export restrictions, retaliation, input disruption, or costly compliance obligations. Positive market reactions become more likely when strategic importance is converted into credible state support, and when investors interpret that support as improving expected cash flows or reducing downside risk. This logic connects industrial-policy scholarship on state support and strategic sectors with finance scholarship on market reactions to information (Fama, 1970; Fama et al., 1969; Mazzucato, 2013, 2021; Rodrik, 2004). The frozen theoretical claim is narrow: Geopolitical competition generates positive market reactions when strategically important firms or sectors are expected to receive credible state support that improves future cash flows or reduces downside risk. This chapter develops that claim without expanding the theory beyond the frozen project logic.

#### 3.2 The Traditional Risk Pathway

The standard expectation in much work on geopolitical risk is that geopolitical competition creates market losses. Rivalry among states can increase uncertainty, disrupt trade and investment, restrict technology flows, and reduce confidence in future earnings. Wars, sanctions, export controls, investment restrictions, and strategic decoupling can all produce risk-off reactions in financial markets (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022; Farrell & Newman, 2019). This risk pathway can be summarised as: Geopolitical Competition → Threat / Restriction / Uncertainty → Negative Market Reaction.

This pathway remains central to the dissertation. The theory does not reject it. In many cases, the threat channel should dominate. Firms exposed to restricted markets, retaliation, input shortages, or compliance costs may lose value. Broad market indices may also fall when geopolitical events increase uncertainty or worsen macroeconomic expectations (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022; Fama, 1970).

The contribution of the dissertation is to identify a second pathway that may operate for selected firms. Geopolitical competition may not only create risks; it may also increase the political value of firms or sectors that governments view as strategically important. When this strategic importance produces credible state support, investors may interpret the event as an opportunity signal rather than only a threat signal (Fama, 1970; Mazzucato, 2013, 2021; Shiller, 2003).

#### 3.3 Geopolitical Competition

Geopolitical competition refers to rivalry among states using military, economic, technological, regulatory, and industrial-policy tools to pursue security, influence, autonomy, or strategic advantage. In this project, the relevant events are not generic diplomatic tensions. The empirical focus is on policy-relevant actions such as subsidies, procurement commitments, export controls, industrial-policy announcements, strategic investment, and supply-chain restrictions. This definition draws on economic statecraft and geoeconomics scholarship that treats markets, trade, finance, and supply chains as instruments and arenas of strategic power (Baldwin, 1985; Blackwill & Harris, 2016; Farrell & Newman, 2019, 2023; Hirschman, 1980; Luttwak, 1990).

Geopolitical competition matters because it changes the political value of economic activity. Technologies, firms, production sites, and supply chains that might otherwise be evaluated mainly in commercial terms can become national-security concerns (Farrell & Newman, 2019, 2023; Weiss, 2014). Semiconductors are a clear example. They are inputs into consumer electronics, cloud computing, artificial intelligence, weapons systems, telecommunications, and critical infrastructure. As geopolitical competition intensifies, governments may treat semiconductor production capacity, design capability, manufacturing equipment, and trusted supply chains as strategic assets.

This does not mean that every firm in a strategic sector benefits. Competition may harm some firms while helping others. The theoretical issue is therefore not whether geopolitical competition is good or bad in aggregate, but how investors distinguish between threatened firms and supported firms within the same broad geopolitical environment (Fama, 1970; Shiller, 2003).

#### 3.4 Strategic Importance as Scope Condition

Strategic importance is the degree to which a firm, sector, technology, or location is viewed by governments as important for national security, technological leadership, economic resilience, military capability, or control over critical supply chains. Strategic importance is a scope condition in the theory. It identifies where states are likely to care, but it does not itself create the market mechanism. This distinction follows from industrial policy and developmental-state scholarship, which shows why states may prioritize strategic sectors without implying that every policy signal immediately produces a positive market reaction (Amsden, 1989, 2001; Chang, 2002; Johnson, 1982; Wade, 1990).

A firm may be strategically important because it controls a critical technology, produces an essential input, occupies a supply-chain chokepoint, operates in a trusted location, or contributes to military and economic resilience (Farrell & Newman, 2019; Weiss, 2014). In semiconductor markets, strategic importance may attach to advanced fabrication, memory production, foundry capacity, semiconductor manufacturing equipment, or supply-chain redundancy.

However, strategic importance is not sufficient for positive market reactions. A strategically important firm may face new restrictions, lose access to foreign markets, incur costly domestic investment obligations, or become exposed to political conditions attached to support. Strategic importance may even increase regulatory scrutiny. For that reason, strategic importance should not be treated as the causal mechanism. It is the condition that makes state support more likely (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022; Fama, 1970).

The dissertation therefore distinguishes clearly between strategic importance and state support. Strategic importance explains why a government may intervene. State support explains why investors may update their expectations about firm value (Fama, 1970; Fama et al., 1969; Rodrik, 2004).

#### 3.5 State Support as Mechanism

State support is the core conversion mechanism in the theory. It refers to government action that plausibly improves the expected economic position of strategically important firms or sectors. It includes subsidies, procurement, grants, tax credits, loans, guarantees, regulation, protection, industrial policy, local-content rules, trusted-supplier rules, and strategic capacity commitments. This mechanism builds on industrial-policy and mission-oriented state arguments that public institutions can shape markets, investment incentives, and innovation trajectories (Block & Keller, 2011; Mazzucato, 2013, 2021; Rodrik, 2004; Weiss, 2014).

State support can affect market expectations through several channels. It may increase expected revenue by creating procurement demand or subsidised capacity expansion. It may lower capital costs by providing grants, loans, guarantees, or tax credits. It may protect demand by favoring trusted suppliers or domestic production. It may reduce downside risk by signaling that the state views the firm or sector as too strategically important to allow failure or decline (Fama, 1970; Mazzucato, 2013, 2021; Rodrik, 2004).

The theory is not that markets mechanically reward all government support. Support can be too small, too anticipated, too conditional, or too costly to generate positive reactions. It may also signal that a firm needs public assistance, which can weaken investor confidence. The mechanism therefore depends on credibility, materiality, directness, and investor interpretation (Fama, 1970; Shiller, 2003).

The strongest version of the mechanism appears when support is specific, funded, and attached to identifiable firms or sectors. Direct named support should provide clearer information to investors than broad policy rhetoric. A named preliminary funding announcement, for example, identifies a beneficiary more clearly than a general statement about national industrial priorities (Fama, 1970; Fama et al., 1969).

#### 3.6 Investor Interpretation

Investor interpretation is the final step before market reaction. Geopolitical events are not automatically classified by markets as either threats or opportunities. They may contain both signals at once. A state-support announcement may imply future subsidies and protected demand, but it may also signal high costs, political constraints, or prior weakness. An export-control event may threaten exposed firms, but it may also imply substitution opportunities for competitors. This interpretive step is consistent with finance theory's emphasis on price reactions to information and behavioral finance's emphasis on narratives and uncertainty in investor judgment (Fama, 1970; Fama et al., 1969; Shiller, 2003).

Investors therefore interpret the event by assessing whether the opportunity signal outweighs the threat signal for particular firms. Positive market reactions should be most likely when the opportunity signal is credible, material, and attached to a visible public-market beneficiary. Negative reactions should be more likely when restrictions, uncertainty, retaliation, or cost burdens dominate (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022; Fama, 1970; Shiller, 2003).

The theory expects heterogeneous reactions. Some firms may gain while others lose around the same event. A named beneficiary may react differently from a sector peer. A broad benchmark may move differently from a firm directly named in a policy announcement. This cross-sectional logic is central to the research design (Brown & Warner, 1985; MacKinlay, 1997).

#### 3.7 Market Reaction

Market reaction refers to short-window changes in firm or sector value around policy-relevant geopolitical events. The dissertation focuses on abnormal returns rather than long-term welfare, policy success, or realised productivity. The outcome captures investor expectations at the time of the event. This approach follows the event-study tradition, which uses short-window price movements to infer the market's assessment of new information (Brown & Warner, 1985; MacKinlay, 1997).

A positive market reaction does not prove that state support will succeed. It does not prove that policy improves social welfare or firm fundamentals in the long run. It shows that investors priced the event in a way consistent with improved expected cash flows, reduced downside risk, protected demand, or other valuation-relevant benefits (Brown & Warner, 1985; Fama, 1970; MacKinlay, 1997).

The theory is therefore a market-expectations theory. It asks whether credible state support under geopolitical competition is visible in short-window market reactions for strategically important firms (Brown & Warner, 1985; MacKinlay, 1997).

#### 3.8 Hypotheses

The dissertation uses the existing frozen hypotheses. H1 State Support Hypothesis: Geopolitical competition events that include credible state support will produce more positive market reactions for affected firms or sectors than events that only increase geopolitical tension. H2 Strategic Importance Scope Hypothesis: State support will have stronger positive market effects when directed toward strategically important firms or sectors than when directed toward less strategically important firms or sectors. H3 Threat Dominance Hypothesis: When geopolitical events impose direct restrictions, lost market access, retaliation risk, or input disruption without credible offsetting state support, threat signals will dominate and market reactions will be negative.H4 Substitution / Reallocation Hypothesis: Geopolitical restrictions will produce positive market reactions for alternative suppliers or trusted-location beneficiaries when the event plausibly redirects demand, sourcing, or investment toward them. H5 Adaptation / Learning Hypothesis: As similar geopolitical-policy events repeat, market reactions will become more differentiated between threatened firms and state-supported or strategically positioned beneficiaries.

These hypotheses preserve the core distinction between threat-dominant events and support-linked opportunity events. They also preserve the expectation that market reactions should vary across firms depending on exposure role, strategic position, and expected state support (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022; Fama, 1970; Mazzucato, 2013, 2021; Shiller, 2003).

#### 3.9 Scope Conditions

The theory should apply most directly in strategic sectors where governments have clear security, resilience, or technological leadership concerns. These include semiconductors, defense, energy security, AI infrastructure, critical minerals, telecommunications, and supply-chain chokepoints. The empirical test in this dissertation focuses on semiconductors because the sector is strategically important, subject to observable state support, represented by public firms and benchmarks, and exposed to both support and threat-dominant events (Farrell & Newman, 2019; Mazzucato, 2013, 2021; Weiss, 2014).

The theory should apply less well to broad market shocks, symbolic policy announcements, unsupported strategic firms, fully anticipated events, or cases where support imposes costs that outweigh benefits. It also should not be extended to claim that geopolitical competition is generally positive for markets (Caldara & Iacoviello, 2022; Fama, 1970; Shiller, 2003).

#### 3.10 Chapter Summary

This chapter has presented the frozen theoretical framework. The central claim is that geopolitical competition can generate positive market reactions for selected firms when strategic importance is converted into credible state support and investors interpret that support as valuation-relevant. The theory is conditional, firm-specific, and limited to market expectations. It does not claim that geopolitical competition is broadly beneficial, that all strategic firms gain, or that state support guarantees long-term success.

The next chapter translates this framework into the research design. It explains the event selection logic, coding protocol, dataset architecture, event-study design, outcome variables, and pre-data commitment used to test whether the observed market reactions are consistent with the State Support mechanism.
